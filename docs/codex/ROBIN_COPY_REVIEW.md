# Robin Copy Review

## Verdict
YELLOW

## One-Sentence Read
EasyLife's voice is calm and mostly mission-fit, but repeated "command center," "premium," "high-tech," and generic suite language is beginning to sound more like internal positioning than clear product copy.

## Mission Voice Fit
The language generally matches the mission: connected, clean, professional, and useful. The strongest copy presents EasyLife as a calm personal operating system for tasks, notes, calendar planning, workouts, projects, and settings. The weakness is repetition. Many surfaces lean on broad positioning words instead of concrete daily-use language, so the product can feel polished but abstract. For this audience, the voice should be practical first, elegant second: show the user what they can do next, then let the tone carry confidence.

## Delicate Wording Risks
- "Command center" appears useful in small doses, but repeated use can make the product sound more intense than calm.
- "Personal operating system" is strong mission language, but it should be used sparingly in UI copy so it does not feel inflated.
- "Premium," "Apple-like," and "high-tech" belong in internal review notes, not product-facing copy.
- "Opening your workspace." is better than plain loading copy, but it may feel slightly vague if used everywhere.
- "EasyLife keeps tasks, notes, calendar planning, workouts, projects, and pipeline work in one command center." is clear, but a little list-heavy and mechanical.
- "Products + demo" is concise but still feels like a navigation shortcut rather than polished hospitality language.
- Repeated "calm," "suite," "connected," and "polished" language risks sounding like a design brief instead of useful interface copy.
- AI experiment copy should stay clearly mock-only and avoid suggesting real intelligence, automation, or backend capability before those exist.

## Beautiful Language Opportunities
- Replace abstract suite claims with daily moments: plan today, capture a note, review what is due, log a workout.
- Make empty states warmer and more specific without becoming cute.
- Use "workspace" carefully as a unifying word across loading, navigation, and signed-in surfaces.
- Give each product a distinct verb set: EasyList triages, EasyNotes captures, EasyCalendar plans, EasyWorkout logs, Settings controls.
- Make marketing hero copy less badge-like and more human by reducing stacked labels.
- Turn generic status language into useful orientation, especially on mobile where space is limited.
- Keep AI Lab language restrained: "mock brief," "draft suggestion," "sample command," and "prototype" are safer than implied real automation.

## Priority Rewrite
The most important wording problem is overuse of internal positioning language in user-facing surfaces. Nami should audit product-facing copy for phrases like "command center," "premium," "high-tech," "operating system," and repeated suite claims, then replace the most visible instances with concrete daily-use language that tells the user what EasyLife helps them do next.

## Suggested Rewrites
- Before: "EasyLife keeps tasks, notes, calendar planning, workouts, projects, and pipeline work in one command center."
  After: "EasyLife brings your tasks, notes, calendar, workouts, and projects into one calm workspace."

- Before: "Opening your workspace."
  After: "Getting your workspace ready."

- Before: "Products + demo"
  After: "Explore EasyLife"

- Before: "command center for the suite"
  After: "control center for your workspace"

- Before: "AI Command Center"
  After: "AI Lab"

- Before: "what matters today"
  After: "what needs attention today"

- Before: "pipeline work"
  After: "projects and follow-ups"

## Voice Rules
- Prefer concrete user actions over abstract product claims.
- Use "workspace" as the main unifying noun; use "command center" rarely.
- Keep product copy calm, useful, and specific.
- Do not use internal taste words like "premium," "high-tech," "Apple-like," or "polished" in UI copy.
- Avoid overpromising AI capability; label prototypes and mock responses clearly.
- Keep buttons short and action-led.
- Empty states should answer: what is here, why it is empty, what to do next.
- Let each app keep a distinct vocabulary while sharing the same calm tone.
