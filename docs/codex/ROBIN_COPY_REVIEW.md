# Robin Copy Review

## Verdict
YELLOW

## One-Sentence Read
EasyLife is moving toward calmer daily-assistant language, but visible remnants of builder, brochure, and command-center wording still interrupt the personal workspace voice.

## Mission Voice Fit
The best new copy fits the mission: "Choose today's next move", "Capture anything", "No school item is saved until you review and add it", and the inbox approval language all feel practical, calm, and trustworthy. The product now sounds more like a daily workspace than a feature catalog in several protected surfaces.

The miss is consistency. Marketing and some app surfaces still use "Command Center", "Demo Path", "Workflow", "handoff", "proof", and "sample" language. Those phrases make EasyLife sound like an internal build, a SaaS brochure, or a reviewer packet instead of one connected personal assistant suite. For this product, the strongest voice is direct and concrete: task list, calendar block, note, reply draft, study task, daily plan, open window.

## Delicate Wording Risks
- "Command Center" remains visible in the command route and navigation language. It sounds generic SaaS and conflicts with the calmer daily-assistant direction.
- "Today Command Center" is especially loud for the protected app. It names the system instead of naming what the user can do next.
- "Demo Path" on marketing pages reads like a builder/reviewer label, not buyer-facing product copy.
- "Workflow" appears in marketing navigation and copy. It is not always wrong, but here it often acts as a vague value prop where "daily flow", "job search", "calendar plan", or "next steps" would be clearer.
- "Handoff" remains in marketing copy and product preview content. It sounds internal and mechanical. Use "Calendar prep", "Task list", "Project brief", or "Action lines" depending on the surface.
- "Proof" appears in visible marketing copy as "visible proof of what got done." It is understandable but slightly salesy and forensic. "Visible record" or "finished work" is warmer.
- "Sample" is mostly internal identifiers, but any visible "sample commands", "sample scan", or "sample data" should become "example" or "practice inbox" if shown to users.
- "Start with..." is improved in Login, but remaining instances should be checked for clarity. It works when the object is concrete, such as a named assignment, but feels instructional when the outcome is vague.
- "School plan without crowding today" is clear but a little self-referential. It describes the UI strategy more than the user's benefit.
- "The things EasyLife would keep from slipping" is charming, but "would" weakens confidence. Since the app is deterministic/local here, say what the section shows rather than implying invisible intelligence.

## Beautiful Language Opportunities
- The protected HQ can become more elegant by using a tight pattern: "Next move", "Today", "Capture", "Open room", "Due work".
- Inbox copy is close to excellent. "Nothing is sent or archived until you approve it" is a strong trust line and should become the standard for approval-based features.
- School planner copy can be warmer and more concrete by naming study load, next assignment, and calendar room without explaining that it is "without crowding today".
- Marketing pages need fewer abstract section labels and more product nouns: "Daily path", "See it in use", "Task flow", "Calendar prep", "Note to task".
- The command layer should sound like quick action, not control-room software. "Quick actions", "Daily review", or "Ask EasyLife" would fit better than "Command Center".
- Empty and low-content states should use plain next steps: "Add one task", "Capture a note", "Place it on the calendar", "Review inbox items".

## Priority Rewrite
Replace remaining visible "Command Center", "Demo Path", "Workflow", "handoff", and "proof" language in customer-facing and protected surfaces with concrete daily-workspace nouns. This should be done as a narrow copy pass, one surface at a time, without changing behavior or adding claims. Nami should prioritize visible JSX text and marketing/navigation labels over internal class names, IDs, route names, and mock-data identifiers.

## Voice Rules
- Use concrete product nouns: task, note, calendar block, reply draft, study task, open window, daily plan, project brief.
- Prefer verbs the user can act on: add, capture, review, approve, plan, finish, place, keep.
- Avoid builder/reviewer language in visible UI: demo, proof, handoff, polish, workflow, command center, sample data.
- Keep first-screen copy short. One next move, one today context line, then compact status.
- Separate marketing copy from app copy. Marketing may explain; the working app should guide action.
- Do not imply real AI, Gmail automation, saving, sending, archiving, or calendar/task creation unless the UI actually does it and requires approval.
- For local/mock surfaces, say "example" only when the user needs to know it is not live data.
- Keep approval language explicit: "Nothing is sent", "Nothing is saved", "Review before adding".
