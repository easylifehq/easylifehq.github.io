# Robin Copy Review

## Verdict
YELLOW

## One-Sentence Read
EasyLife is calmer and more concrete than before, but several visible strings still sound like internal demo guidance or generic product framing instead of a trusted daily workspace.

## Mission Voice Fit
The language mostly fits a connected personal productivity suite: "daily plan", "task list", "workspace", "calendar", "notes", and "capture" are all in the right family. The weakest areas are the public product pages and metadata, where "command center", "Demo Path", "Show this first", "Workflow", "handoff", "proof", and repeated "Open EasyLife" labels pull the voice back toward builder language and brochure staging. The protected app is closer to the mission when it names the next user action directly.

## Delicate Wording Risks
- "command center" in public metadata overstates the product and sounds generic SaaS rather than personal and calm.
- "Demo Path" and "Show this first" are customer-facing builder instructions. They tell the site owner how to present the product, not the buyer what they can do.
- "Use these moments to explain the product without wandering through every setting" is explicitly presenter-facing and should not appear on a public product surface.
- "Draft handoff to EasyList", "EasyList handoff", and "Capture, refine, then hand off" use internal process language. Guests/users need "task draft", "send to tasks", or "review in EasyList".
- "Workflow" is overused as a navigation label and value word. It is vague unless the user, action, and outcome are clear.
- "Keep visible proof of what got done" sounds defensive and slightly salesy. "Keep completed tasks visible" is plainer and more trustworthy.
- "Open EasyLife" repeats across product pages and reads generic. Context-specific action labels would feel more intentional.
- "AI review should use only..." and "If AI is unavailable..." may be appropriate in Settings, but the copy needs careful staging so it reads as a user control, not a system requirement note.

## Beautiful Language Opportunities
- Replace public product-page staging copy with user outcomes: "Start with your day", "Review the plan", "Capture a note", "Send action lines to Tasks".
- Give each module one concrete first action instead of repeating suite language: Tasks can say "Add today's first task"; Notes can say "Start a note"; Calendar can say "Plan today's blocks"; Workout can say "Log a session".
- Change "handoff" language to warmer, clearer connection language: "send to Tasks", "turn into task drafts", "review action lines".
- Make metadata quieter and more useful. A label like "Recent note" or "Calendar prep" beats a chip that sounds like product proof.
- Public pages should separate marketing from app copy: explain the product briefly, then let preview rows read like real app state.
- Settings copy can become more reassuring by naming the user's control: "When AI is unavailable, keep manual planning available."

## Priority Rewrite
The most important fix is removing customer-facing builder/demo language from the shared product marketing component. "Demo Path", "Show this first", and the explanatory sentence beneath it break the buyer voice and make the page feel like instructions for a presentation. Replace that section with user-facing language that describes a normal path through the product, such as "A simple way through" and "Start with one useful action, then review the connected next step."

## Voice Rules
- Use concrete daily-life nouns: task, note, calendar block, daily plan, workout log, project brief, setting.
- Keep first-screen labels short and action-shaped.
- Do not expose builder language on buyer surfaces: avoid demo, proof, handoff, polish, workflow, show this first, and explain the product.
- Use "send to", "turn into", "review", "capture", "plan", and "log" instead of process words.
- Marketing pages may explain the product, but working app screens should tell the user what to do next.
- Avoid broad claims like command center, operating system, premium, or high-tech unless the surface proves the claim immediately.
- Keep AI copy humble and conditional: show what is reviewed, what stays manual, and what requires user approval.
