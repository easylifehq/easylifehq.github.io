# Robin Copy Review

## Verdict
YELLOW

## One-Sentence Read
EasyLife is moving toward a calmer daily assistant voice, but several surfaces still sound like product scaffolding or broad suite marketing instead of a concrete place to decide what to do next.

## Mission Voice Fit
The language mostly fits the mission when it names daily objects: tasks, notes, calendar planning, follow-ups, study load, workout log, and workspace. It weakens when it returns to broad claims like "command center," repeated product explanation, or module-page hero language that tells the user what EasyLife is instead of helping them act. For a connected personal operating system, the strongest copy should feel quiet, direct, and useful inside the signed-in app: one next move, today context, and clear module signals.

## Delicate Wording Risks
- "command center" in public metadata still feels generic SaaS and too broad for the personal assistant promise.
- "Start Free" and "See Features" style module-page CTAs, as described in Simon's review, keep the public surfaces in sales-page mode instead of calm product doorway mode.
- Repeated module identity copy can make EasyLife feel like several separate products rather than one suite.
- "proof," "demo," and "sample" hits appear mostly in internal identifiers, but any visible use should be replaced. These words make the product feel unfinished or staged.
- "School planner" is clear, but if overused above the fold it risks making the HQ feel like a feature inventory rather than a today surface.
- "approval queue" is understandable for staff-style software, but for EasyLife it may sound too operational unless paired with user language like "Review email suggestions."
- Any "draft reply" copy must keep the approval boundary plain. It should never imply EasyLife sends, archives, or acts without the user's choice.
- Feature explanations appearing too early are a staging problem even when the sentences are individually clear.

## Beautiful Language Opportunities
- Replace broad suite labels with daily-life nouns: "today plan," "next task," "calendar block," "study load," "follow-up," "draft reply," and "recent note."
- Make HQ copy more human and less architectural: "Start with this" is stronger than "workspace overview."
- Use compact status language for modules: "2 deadlines," "1 draft reply," "Open evening block," "Heavy study day."
- Public product pages can feel more premium by saying less: name the product, say what it helps with, show the preview, then move details lower.
- Email intelligence copy should emphasize review and consent: "Review before adding" and "Draft only" are clear, trust-building phrases.
- School planning copy can become more useful by naming the student action: "Block study time," "Add assignment," "Review exam week."

## Priority Rewrite
The most important wording problem is the remaining broad product framing, especially "command center" and marketing-style module hero copy. Replace it with concrete today-language that names the user's next action and outcome. Nami should prioritize one visible surface at a time: public metadata first if it is customer-facing, then HQ/module first-screen strings that still describe the system instead of the user's work.

## Voice Rules
- Lead with the user's next action, not the product category.
- Use concrete nouns: task list, calendar block, note, follow-up, draft reply, study load, workout log, project brief.
- Avoid broad product fog: command center, proof, demo, sample data, polish, handoff, platform, automation.
- Keep first-screen labels short and action-ready.
- Put explanations below the working surface or behind a deliberate click.
- Separate public promise from app copy. Public pages may explain; signed-in pages should help the user act.
- For email and assistant-like surfaces, make approval and non-sending behavior explicit.
- Do not imply prediction, autonomous action, AI judgment, or backend capability unless the visible product actually does it.
