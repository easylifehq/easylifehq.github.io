# Robin Copy Review

## Verdict
YELLOW

## One-Sentence Read
EasyLife is moving toward a calmer assistant voice, but visible copy still carries too much suite, demo, command-center, and internal-process language for a product that should feel like a working daily companion.

## Mission Voice Fit
The strongest language now points toward Today, Capture, Plan, Notes, More, daily plan, workspace, and concrete next actions. That fits the mission: one connected personal system that helps the user act quickly. The risk is residue from older framing. Phrases like "command center", "demo", "proof", "sample data", and repeated product-module framing make the app sound like it is presenting itself rather than helping the signed-in user decide what to do next.

## Delicate Wording Risks
- "command center" appears in public metadata and still sounds generic SaaS rather than personal assistant.
- "demo" in visible or near-visible app paths risks making the product feel fake, temporary, or staged.
- "proof" and "auth-proof-card" are likely internal class naming, but any visible "proof" language should be avoided unless it names concrete evidence for a user.
- "sample data" and "sample" identifiers are acceptable as internal data labels, but should not surface as guest/user-facing copy.
- "Products and demo" style language has been repaired in places, but the marketing system still appears to carry product-catalog habits.
- "personal operating system" belongs in mission docs, not routine UI. In-product copy should say what the user can do today.
- Public and protected surfaces are still too close in voice. Marketing can explain the suite; the working app should use direct task labels.
- The app should avoid telling the user about "modules" or "suite" before the daily job is clear.
- Product-page hero copy risks explaining the whole system at once instead of staging the promise, action, and preview.
- Settings copy still risks showing account/config detail before the practical control-center job is established.

## Beautiful Language Opportunities
- Use "Today" as the emotional and functional anchor: short, direct, and concrete.
- Let Capture sound like a trusted intake surface: "Add a task", "Save a note", "Turn this into a plan".
- Make Plan language sharper with calendar nouns: "next block", "open time", "today's plan", "calendar block".
- Make Notes feel like memory without overclaiming intelligence: "recent notes", "review later", "turn into action".
- Replace product-catalog copy with working-life nouns: task list, daily plan, calendar block, note, follow-up, workout log.
- Keep public marketing warmer, but keep app copy spare and operational.
- Use empty states to answer: what is this for, what should I do, what do I get?
- Make More feel curated, not hidden: "More tools" is better than a feature inventory.

## Priority Rewrite
The single most important wording problem is remaining internal/product-demo language on customer-facing and protected surfaces. Nami should create one copy-only task to replace one visible instance at a time of "command center", "demo", "proof", "sample data", "suite", or broad "module" framing with concrete daily-action language, starting with metadata and the protected shell/HQ first viewport.

- Before: "module status"

- Before: "personal operating system"
  After: "daily workspace"

## Voice Rules
- Use concrete daily-life nouns before product nouns.
- In the protected app, label actions before explaining features.
- Keep first-screen copy short: one next move, one today read, one capture path.
- Avoid "demo", "proof", "handoff", "polish", "workflow", "command center", and "personal operating system" in visible UI.
- Marketing may explain the product; app screens should help the user act.
- Do not claim AI, automation, prediction, or smart behavior unless the visible UI proves it.
- Use "Today", "Capture", "Plan", "Notes", and "More" consistently as the primary mental model.
- Keep optional tools secondary without making them sound discarded or hidden.
